## Narration Script

[Warm, informative, professional voice — like a therapist explaining to a new client]

EMDR — Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing — is a psychotherapy approach developed by Dr. Francine Shapiro in 1987. It's one of the most extensively researched trauma treatments available, recognized by the World Health Organization, the American Psychological Association, and the Department of Veterans Affairs.

[pause 2s]

So how does it work?

EMDR uses bilateral stimulation — typically side-to-side eye movements, but also alternating sounds or taps — to help your brain reprocess distressing memories. The theory, called Adaptive Information Processing, suggests that traumatic experiences can get stuck in the brain, stored in a raw, unprocessed form. The bilateral stimulation mimics the rapid eye movement phase of sleep — REM sleep — helping your brain digest these stuck memories and file them away as ordinary past events.

[pause 2s]

A typical EMDR session follows eight phases.

Phase one is history taking — understanding your background and what you'd like to work on.

Phase two is preparation — learning grounding and self-soothing techniques.

Phase three is assessment — identifying a specific target memory along with the negative belief, emotions, and body sensations attached to it.

Phase four is the core work — desensitization — where bilateral stimulation is used while you focus on the memory.

Phase five is installation — strengthening a positive, adaptive belief to replace the old negative one.

Phase six is a body scan — checking for residual tension.

Phase seven is closure — returning to equilibrium.

And phase eight is re-evaluation — checking progress next time.

[pause 2s]

Research support for EMDR is strong. Over sixty controlled studies have been published since 1989. fMRI scans show that EMDR reduces activity in the amygdala — the brain's alarm center — and strengthens the prefrontal cortex, which helps with rational thinking and emotional regulation.

[pause 2s]

EMDR is helpful for trauma and PTSD, phobias, panic attacks, grief, performance anxiety, and childhood abuse. And here's something important: unlike traditional talk therapy, EMDR doesn't require you to describe every detail of your trauma. You simply focus on the memory while following bilateral stimulation, and the processing happens naturally.

[pause 2s]

Some people feel temporary emotional or physical sensations as memories are reprocessed. This is normal — it's part of the healing process.

[slightly slower]

This information is for educational purposes. For complex trauma or PTSD, please work with a licensed EMDR-trained therapist.

[END]
