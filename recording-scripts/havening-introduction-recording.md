## Narration Script

[Warm, informative, gentle]

Havening — also called Amygdala Depotentiation Technique — is a psychosensory therapy developed by Dr. Ronald Ruden in the 2000s. It uses gentle, soothing touch — specifically stroking the arms, face, and palms — combined with directed attention and distraction to depotentiate, or unlock, traumatic memories at the neurological level.

[pause 2s]

Here's how it works. Havening targets the amygdala — your brain's threat-detection center. When a traumatic or distressing memory is recalled, the amygdala activates a stress response. Havening uses delta wave stimulation via gentle touch to generate calming delta brainwaves that override the amygdala's alarm signal.

[pause 2s]

There are three core elements. First, Havening touch — gentle, repetitive stroking of the upper arms, palms, and face. Second, active attention — focusing on the distressing memory or sensation, which activates the amygdala. Third, distraction — while touching, you're guided through a distraction technique like humming, counting, or eye movements, which prevents conscious re-traumatization.

The combination creates a window where the memory can be reconsolidated without the emotional charge.

[pause 2s]

The Havening touch areas are simple. Upper arms — gentle stroking from shoulder to elbow, like comforting a child. Palms — slow circular strokes with your thumbs. And face — gentle sweeping strokes across the forehead, cheeks, or temples. You can self-Haven or be Havened by another person.

[pause 2s]

Research support includes a 2010 clinical case series by Dr. Ruden showing rapid resolution of PTSD and phobia symptoms. fMRI studies show Havening reduces amygdala hyperactivity and increases prefrontal regulation. And it's growing in clinical use among trauma therapists and EMDR practitioners.

[pause 2s]

Who benefits from Havening? People with trauma and PTSD, phobias and panic attacks, intrusive memories and flashbacks, acute stress and overwhelm, sensitivity to criticism or rejection, and performance anxiety.

[pause 2s]

Havening feels soothing — like being comforted. Sessions are shorter than traditional therapy, typically 5 to 15 minutes for a specific trigger. Processing can feel gentle but profound. Some people report feeling more relaxed after a single session.

This information is for educational purposes. For complex trauma or PTSD, work with a licensed Havening practitioner.

[END]
