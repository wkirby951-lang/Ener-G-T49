## Narration Script

[Warm, informative, accessible — like a wellness coach explaining to a newcomer]

EFT — Emotional Freedom Technique — often called tapping, is a mind-body therapy that combines acupressure with cognitive reframing. It was developed in the 1990s by Gary Craig as a simplified form of Thought Field Therapy.

[pause 2s]

Here's how it works. EFT is based on the idea that emotional distress is a disruption in the body's energy system. By tapping on specific acupressure endpoints while mentally focusing on a problem, you send a calming signal to the amygdala — your brain's fear center — and help rewire the stress response.

[pause 2s]

The basic sequence is simple.

First — identify the issue and rate its intensity from 0 to 10.

Second — create a setup statement that acknowledges the problem while affirming self-acceptance.

Third — tap through a standard sequence of meridian points while repeating a reminder phrase.

And fourth — re-rate the intensity. Keep tapping until the number drops to 0 or a manageable level.

[pause 2s]

There are nine main tapping points. Starting with the karate chop point — the side of your hand. Then the eyebrow, side of eye, under eye, under nose, chin, collarbone, under arm, and the top of your head.

Each point targets a different aspect of your emotional experience — fear, anger, anxiety, shame, overwhelm, and so on.

[pause 2s]

What does the research say? A 2010 study by Church and colleagues showed that EFT significantly reduced cortisol levels — the stress hormone — in a single session. A large 2019 meta-analysis by Feinstein found that EFT had large effect sizes for anxiety, depression, and PTSD — comparable to cognitive behavioral therapy. And fMRI studies show tapping reduces activity in the amygdala.

[pause 2s]

Who benefits from EFT? People with anxiety and stress, phobias, PTSD, chronic pain, insomnia, performance anxiety, and food cravings. It's gentle enough for daily self-care and can be done anywhere — no equipment needed.

[pause 2s]

Some people feel immediate relief. Others need a few rounds. It's common to experience a yawn, a sigh, or a deep breath as the stress releases.

This information is for educational purposes. For complex trauma or diagnosed conditions, work with a licensed mental health professional.

[END]
