# EMDR — Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing

## What Is EMDR?

EMDR is a psychotherapy modality developed by Dr. Francine Shapiro in 1987. It is one of the most extensively researched trauma treatments available, recognized by the World Health Organization (WHO), the American Psychological Association (APA), and the Department of Veterans Affairs as an evidence-based treatment for trauma and PTSD.

### How It Works

EMDR uses **bilateral stimulation** — typically side-to-side eye movements, but also alternating sounds or taps — to help the brain reprocess distressing memories. The theory behind EMDR (called **Adaptive Information Processing**) suggests that traumatic experiences can get "stuck" in the brain, stored in a raw, unprocessed form. The bilateral stimulation mimics the rapid eye movement (REM) phase of sleep, helping the brain "digest" these stuck memories and file them away as ordinary past events.

### What Happens in a Session

A typical EMDR session follows an 8-phase protocol:

1. **History Taking** — Understanding your background and what you'd like to work on
2. **Preparation** — Learning grounding and self-soothing techniques
3. **Assessment** — Identifying a specific target memory, along with the negative belief, emotions, and body sensations attached to it
4. **Desensitization** — The core reprocessing phase using bilateral stimulation
5. **Installation** — Strengthening a positive, adaptive belief to replace the old negative one
6. **Body Scan** — Checking for residual tension or distress
7. **Closure** — Returning to equilibrium at the end of each session
8. **Re-evaluation** — Checking progress at the start of the next session

### Research Support

- **Shapiro (1989):** Original controlled study showing EMDR significantly reduced anxiety and trauma symptoms
- **Van Etten & Taylor (1998):** Meta-analysis found EMDR and exposure therapy equally effective for PTSD
- **Bisson et al. (2007):** WHO guidelines recommended EMDR as one of three evidence-based trauma treatments
- **Pagani et al. (2012):** fMRI studies showed EMDR produces measurable changes in brain activity in the amygdala, hippocampus, and prefrontal cortex

### Who Benefits

EMDR is helpful for:
- Trauma and PTSD
- Phobias and panic attacks
- Grief and loss
- Performance anxiety
- Childhood abuse or neglect
- Accident or injury survivors
- First responders and veterans

### What to Expect

Unlike traditional talk therapy, EMDR doesn't require you to describe every detail of your trauma. You focus on the memory while following bilateral stimulation, and the processing happens naturally. Some people feel temporary emotional or physical sensations as memories are reprocessed — this is normal and part of the healing process.
